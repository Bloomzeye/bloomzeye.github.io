As an art response to the Global Day of Ceasefire for Palestine, on December 11th, 2023, I made a game called [Watermelon Match](https://bloomzeye.itch.io/watermelon-match), as a free browser game on itch.io.

I've talked and learned about Palestine in many of my creative mediums; clay, zines, crochet, sewing, digital art, writing, and more. 

However, I was stumped on how to talk about Palestine within my game development. 

Video games are used for entertainment, escapism, fun, and playfulness, but none of those adjectives were appropriate at this time.

Granted, that's not all of what games are, but I didn't want to be careless on how I was trying to tie my game development in with my response to current events.

Eventually, I realized that I could use art research to learn the significance of the designs I would create, which would give me the chance to find resources to learn more about Palestinian resistance, people, and culture.

![Alt text: A graphic design of a title header reads: "My design research process", written in a black, bubbly pixel art font. There's a watermelon patterned decorative top-border, and a thin green border arond the entire title header.](\images\blog-headers\watermelon-match\researchHeader.webp)

### My design research process

My game and story ideas tend to grow either from a sketch, doodle, or a sentence. 

By using photo references of watermelons from Unsplash; a free stock photo website, and learning about the keffiyeh and the history behind the pattern, art research awards me the opportunity to learn the meaning behind the art I'm creating, and to understand the humanity from what others have created.

Collecting references for inspiration, using stock photo websites, thrifted books, libraries, videos, internet archives, talking and listening to others, and even my own photos, is all apart of my game design process.

When I take the time to slow down and build a foundation for my visuals and designs, my work feels more unique to my own creativity and imagination.

![Alt text: A graphic design of a title header reads: "My art process", written in a black, bubbly pixel art font. There's a watermelon patterned decorative top-border, and a thin green border arond the entire title header.](\images\blog-headers\watermelon-match\artHeader.webp)

### My art process

Since this game concept was so simple, I pretty much went straight to Aseprite; a pixel art program, that I've been using since my start in game development.

![Alt text: A set of three pixel art watermelons; one whole, half, and quarter-sliced, next to a pixel pattern of the keffiyeh, with a thin green border arond the art.](\images\blog-headers\watermelon-match\watermelonPixels2.webp)

I limited myself to about 2-5 art assets, and no animations. Since I only had a watermelon, the keffiyeh as a background pattern, and a simple heart for my particle effects, I was ready for programming within a few hours after I got started. 

![Alt text: A graphic design of a title header reads: "Using a game engine", written in a black, bubbly pixel art font. There's a watermelon patterned decorative top-border, and a thin green border arond the entire title header.](\images\blog-headers\watermelon-match\engineHeader.webp)

### Core gameplay mechanics

This is where creating small games prototypes ***saved*** my pixelated behind. 

My previous practice project was a simple drag and drop prototype, where the player matches up color boxes with its like-colored container. Essentially, I just changed the art assets and called it a day, using my own practice project as a template. 

Once I completed my main programing, I did whatever quick bug fixes I could, added a ‚Äúwin screen‚Äù that doubled as my ‚Äúabout me‚Äù, and published my game to my itch.io page.

It‚Äôs easier daydreamed than done, to take an idea and turn it into an actual game, but I‚Äôm always happy when I do.

### What I've learned

Making small game prototypes has helped me learn foundational mechanics, refine my workflow, familiarize myself with the game engine I‚Äôm using, see a project to completion, and grow my confidence as a game designer and developer. 

Whenever I create practice projects, it allows me to develop out of curiosity instead of a having a "deadline" mindset. I‚Äôm able to troubleshoot at an easier pace, take my time of exploring concepts and ideas, and just have fun.

When I take my time to learn a new mechanic, (try to) read the documentation, and ask for help when I need it (and ideally long before I‚Äôm desperate for it), I become more confident as a game developer.
